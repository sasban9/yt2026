A mindset shift

Two ways to face
every problem.

Most people are trained to describe what's wrong.
Problem-solvers and policy-makers are trained to ask
what comes next.
This is how you make that shift.

The default mode

Most people live inside the problem.

When something goes wrong, the natural human reaction is to stay near the pain — analysing it, naming it, sharing it, and waiting for someone else to fix it.

The pattern

"The system is broken."  ·  "Nobody cares."  ·  "This always happens."  ·  "There's nothing I can do."

Side by side

Same situation.
Opposite orientations.

Problem-oriented
  • Describes what is wrong
  • Looks backward for cause
  • Assigns blame
  • Waits for permission
  • Measures suffering
Solution-oriented
  • Defines what success looks like
  • Looks forward for leverage
  • Assigns ownership
  • Acts within their control
  • Measures progress
Context

Problem orientation is
wired in. It's not a character flaw.

The brain's negativity bias evolved to detect threats — not opportunities. Dwelling on problems kept us alive. But in modern work and life, it keeps us stuck.

"We are not designed to think forward.
We are designed to survive."
Shift 1 of 5

Start with language.

The words you use shape the thoughts you can have. Problem-oriented language closes options. Solution-oriented language opens them.

"We can't"
→
"How might we…"
"The problem is…"
→
"The goal is…"
"Yes, but…"
→
"Yes, and…"
Shift 2 of 5

Replace the statement
with a question.

Every complaint contains a hidden question. The shift is learning to hear it — and ask it out loud instead of stopping at the complaint.

?

"Traffic is terrible" → "What route or time changes would save me 20 minutes?"

The complaint becomes a design brief.

?

"This team doesn't communicate" → "What would good communication look like here?"

The blame becomes a standard to aim for.

?

"Nobody follows up" → "What system would make follow-up automatic?"

The frustration becomes a process problem worth solving.

Shift 3 of 5

Stop asking who's responsible.
Ask what's possible.

Problem-thinkers fixate on the gap between what is and who should have fixed it. Solution-thinkers fixate on the gap between what is and what could be — and what they specifically can do.

The reframe

Not: "Whose fault is this?"
But: "What is the one thing within my control that would move this forward?"

Ownership is not about blame. It's about choosing to act inside your circle of influence.

Shift 4 of 5

You can't solve
what you haven't defined.

Most problem-oriented thinking fails because it's vague. "This is bad" is not a problem statement. A clear, specific definition of the desired outcome is the foundation of all problem-solving.

1

Name the current state precisely

Not "things are bad" — but what specifically, by how much, affecting whom.

2

Name the desired state precisely

What does success look like? By when? Measured how?

3

The gap between them is your real problem

Now you have something actionable — not a complaint, but a brief.

Shift 5 of 5

Don't wait for the
perfect solution.

Problem-oriented thinkers wait — for permission, for resources, for the right moment. Solution-oriented thinkers start with the smallest viable action and build from there.

Perfect plan
→
Small move now
The policy-maker's rule

Every great policy and every great product started as a rough idea someone chose to act on before they were fully ready. Motion creates information. Waiting creates anxiety.

A practical tool

Six questions that move
any conversation forward.

💡

What outcome do we want?

Define success before diagnosing failure.

🔍

What's already working?

Build on strengths, not just gaps.

⚡

What's the smallest next step?

Action over analysis paralysis.

🎯

Who owns this?

Assign a person, not a department.

📅

By when?

A decision without a deadline is a wish.

📊

How will we know it worked?

Define the signal of success upfront.

The highest level

Policy-makers don't solve problems.
They design conditions for solutions.

The best leaders don't fix every problem themselves. They build systems, incentives, and environments where problems get solved by default.

Reactive thinker
  • Responds to each crisis as it arrives
  • Puts out fires personally
  • Thinks case by case
  • Busy, always overwhelmed
Systems thinker
  • Identifies patterns across problems
  • Builds rules that prevent fires
  • Thinks in policies and processes
  • Leveraged, increasingly free
The shift, summarised

The move isn't from
negativity to positivity.

It's from description to design. Anyone can describe what's wrong. The rare skill is asking "what next?" — and meaning it.

Change your language
Ask better questions
Own your circle
Define the win
Act before ready
Design for scale

You don't need a title to be solution-oriented.
You just need to make it a habit.
